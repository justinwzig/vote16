Hi there!

We have an ambitious vision for the future...

We envision a future in which voting is second nature and where students and youth are excited
about politics -- they participate in political organizations in school and outside it,
advocate for their rights and their futures, and are able to exercise the most basic
right of a democracy: voting.

See how we plan to get there.

Our Mission

Our goals include...

1. Create public support

Creating​ a critical mass of public support for lowering the voting age in local elections, particularly among youth, through civics classes, youth political organizations, and social Media.

2. Advocate for change.

Advocating​ for the passage of city council resolutions in support of lowering the municipal voting age, leveraging grassroots youth support.

3. Engage State Representatives

Engaging​ state representatives in the creation and introduction of the necessary state legislation to expand the vote.

4. Harness the power of voting

Harnessing​ the power of habitual voting and civic engagement to decrease voter apathy in the long-term.

FAQ & Research

We get asked some questions pretty frequently -- here's some in-depth answers.

How can lowering the voting age make a difference?

Research shows that voting is habitual. By lowering the voting age to 16, we increase the possibility that a person will vote in the next election by 25 percent. The earlier people start voting, the more likely they are to become lifelong voters.

Why are people any more likely to start voting at 16 than 18?

Because 16 falls within a better developmental stage to start building voting habits. Age 18 is tough: many 18-year-olds are in the midst of major life transitions that make it a difficult time to take on the daunting prospect of registration, especially if they are moving out of their home state, and deciding how to vote. At 16, however, students are searching for ways to broaden their influence on the world, and are still a part of the communities in which they grew up, something that has been demonstrated to change both how people think about politics and the likeliness that they go to the polls.

Will 16- and 17-year-olds vote when given the chance?

Increasingly, youth are getting involved in political organizations across the country in their high schools and communities, indicating a broader interest in political engagement. This extends beyond just those youth who are directly involved in political clubs, as these organizations frequently involve initiatives to register voters and encourage others outside the club to participate in civic life.

Since the local voting age was lowered in Takoma Park, Maryland in 2013, 16- and 17-year-olds are turning out to vote at a rate about four times greater than the overall turnout.

Are 16- and 17-year-olds mature enough to vote?

18-year-olds don’t have a sudden increase in political understanding. In fact, the most dramatic change in reasoning ability happens around puberty. At 16 and 17, we are ready and able to make thoughtful political decisions.
